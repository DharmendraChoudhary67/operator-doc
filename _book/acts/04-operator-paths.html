The Workstreams We Actually Explored

This section is not theory. These were the real implementation and study tracks we used to understand the problem from different angles.

Why We Needed Multiple Tracks

If we had looked at only one operator, we would have missed too much:

  • the no-operator path teaches raw mechanics
  • OpsTree shows what a working production-style operator feels like
  • our scratch-built operator shows how the control flow changes when we own the CRD, controller, annotations, and webhook
  • the official operator shows what an upstream architecture looks like when it already has ValkeyCluster and internal ValkeyNode objects

That is why this booklet treats them as workstreams, not as random options in a table.

Workstream 1: No-Operator

This path kept the mechanics exposed:

  • manual cluster formation
  • direct Kubernetes objects
  • shell-driven operational steps
  • minimal abstraction hiding what happens

This was useful because it forced us to learn:

  • what resources really have to exist
  • how pods come up
  • what the cluster needs before it can form
  • where day-two work becomes painful without controller logic

Workstream 2: OpsTree

OpsTree gave us a working operator-shaped reference:

  • cluster CRD
  • controller-managed resources
  • StatefulSets
  • PVC-backed workloads
  • a more realistic operational lifecycle

This helped us move from raw mechanics to a more practical operator model.

Workstream 3: Our Scratch-Built Operator

This track mattered because we moved from studying operators to building one ourselves.

The important parts were:

  • our own ValkeyCluster CRD
  • our own controller logic
  • generated ConfigMap, Service, StatefulSet, and job resources
  • annotation-based signaling for shard anti-affinity
  • a mutating webhook that patches the final pod spec before scheduling

This is where the design became concrete, because we had to decide exactly where each policy belonged.

Workstream 4: Official Valkey Operator Plus Our Changes

The official operator already had a strong base:

  • ValkeyCluster
  • internal ValkeyNode
  • cluster bootstrap logic
  • rolling updates
  • degraded-state recovery
  • TLS and ACL support
  • support for both StatefulSet and Deployment

Our work here was not to throw that away. It was to identify the missing production-facing pieces and shape them in a way that fits the existing architecture.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Path Day 1 Day 2 Transparency Production fit What it taught us
No-operator simple to start heavier to maintain highest weaker long-term forced us to understand every moving part
OpsTree more setup upfront calmer operations medium strong showed a practical operator-first workflow
Scratch-built operator highest ownership burden flexible if maintained well high depends on long-term commitment let us encode webhook and shard-affinity behavior ourselves
Official operator plus our design direction good upstream base stronger if gaps are closed medium potentially strongest showed where PVC handling and shard-safe placement need to grow

What These Workstreams Taught Us

Together, the four paths gave us a complete picture:

  • no-operator taught us the raw mechanics
  • OpsTree showed what a mature operator workflow feels like
  • the scratch-built path showed where to place our own control hooks
  • the official path showed where upstream can be extended instead of replaced
